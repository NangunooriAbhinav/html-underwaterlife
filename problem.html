PROBLEM
STATEMENTS.

TOXICITY

Polluted water can contain a range of toxic substances, including heavy metals, pesticides, and industrial chemicals. These substances can be harmful to aquatic organisms, causing illness, disease, or death.

One of the most common ways water pollution leads to toxicity is through the release of heavy metals like lead, mercury, and cadmium into water bodies. These metals are toxic to living organisms, and when they accumulate in the food chain, they can cause serious health problems in humans, such as damage to the nervous system and brain.

HABITAT

Pollution can degrade or destroy important habitats for aquatic organisms, such as coral reefs or wetlands, making it difficult or impossible for these organisms to survive.

When excess nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff and wastewater discharge enter the ocean, they can fuel the growth of algae and other plants. As these plants die and decompose, the bacteria that break them down consume oxygen from the water, leading to a decrease in oxygen levels in the affected areas. This can create "dead zones," areas of water with little to no oxygen, where most marine life cannot survive.

OXYGEN

Polluted water can lead to reduced oxygen levels in the water, making it difficult for aquatic organisms to breathe. This can result in suffocation and death of fish and other marine life.

When excess nutrients like nitrogen and phosphorus from agricultural runoff and wastewater discharge enter the ocean, they can fuel the growth of algae and other plants. As these plants die and decompose, the bacteria that break them down consume oxygen from the water, leading to a decrease in oxygen levels in the affected areas. This can create "dead zones," areas of water with little to no oxygen, where most marine life cannot survive.

FOOD WEB

Water pollution can alter the food web in aquatic ecosystems, disrupting the natural balance and leading to declines in populations of certain species.

One of the primary ways that marine pollution affects food webs is through the bioaccumulation of toxins in the tissues of marine organisms. When pollutants such as heavy metals, pesticides, and industrial chemicals enter the ocean, they can be absorbed by small organisms like plankton. As larger organisms consume these small organisms, the toxins become more concentrated in their tissues, leading to a process called biomagnification. This means that predators at the top of the food chain, such as sharks and dolphins, can accumulate very high levels of toxins in their bodies, leading to serious health problems and even death.
